1 · Executive Summary

GOConditional · 90-Day Test

"OpsBrief solves a recurring, time-boxed pain that the founder lives daily. The market is large enough for a bootstrapped business but not VC-scale. The dominant risk is not demand — it is integration breadth. Recommendation: 90-day validation sprint targeting 25 customer-discovery calls, then a no-code MVP for the first 10 paying users at $20/mo before any platform build."

The opportunity sits at the intersection of three observed trends: (1) AI-generated daily briefings have crossed the credibility threshold for routine business use; (2) SMBs (50–500 employees) increasingly run their analytics out of a fragmented stack — Sheets + a warehouse + Slack + an ad-hoc dashboard tool — that nobody is gluing together; and (3) the analyst role at this tier is overweight on report production and underweight on judgment. OpsBrief reclaims the first 90 minutes of every analyst's day and converts it into manager-ready summary output.

2 · Problem Validation

Pain Frequency

Daily. Every business day, an SMB analyst opens the same 3–6 dashboards, copies yesterday's metrics into a Slack message or email, writes "no anomalies" or "X is down 4% — looking into it," and sends it to their manager.

Pain Intensity

Medium-high. Not catastrophic. But it consumes the analyst's freshest 90 minutes on the lowest-leverage work of their day, and it pushes the actual analysis they were hired to do into post-lunch fatigue hours.

Today's Workaround

Manual. Some teams build Looker dashboards or Hex notebooks but those still require the analyst to open them, read them, and write the summary. There is no "give me yesterday in 6 sentences" product in this tier.

Willingness to Pay Signal

Indirect but real: SMBs already pay $30–$80/seat for Looker, Hex, Sigma, Mode. A $20–$40/seat briefing layer that sits on top is a price increment they can absorb without procurement review.

Verdict: Problem is recurring, time-boxed, and unaddressed by current dashboard tooling. The pain is not "I don't have data" — it is "I have too many places where the data lives and I am the integration layer."

6 · Market Gap Analysis

The Wedge

No incumbent owns the SMB daily briefing use case. Every tool above either targets a larger company or assumes the analyst will compose the summary, not receive it. The wedge is "the briefing your manager wants to read at 8:55 a.m." — not "another place to build a chart."

Why It's Still Open

Mid-market tools price out SMBs ($30–60/seat × 50-person plans is a procurement event). Single-source AI features (Sheets+Gemini) cannot triangulate across the typical SMB's fragmented stack. The SMB tier is too small for enterprise vendors to bother with the integrations.

8 · Buyer Persona

"Maya — The Lone Analyst"

28 years old. Operations Analyst at a 180-person logistics SaaS. Three years out of a Business Analytics master's. Reports to a VP of Ops who joined from McKinsey and expects a Slack-message briefing every morning by 9 a.m. ET. Owns 4 dashboards in Hex, 7 sheets in Google Drive, a Snowflake read connection, and a Slack channel called #daily-ops. Spends her first 60–90 minutes opening tabs, copying numbers, and writing the same sentence structure she wrote yesterday.

What she actually wants: the next promotion. What is in her way: she does not have time to do analysis, only reporting. Every minute spent refreshing dashboards is a minute not spent on the kind of work that gets her recognized.

9 · Customer Pain Points

Pain 1 · Time tax

60–90 minutes/day reclaiming the same numbers across 4–6 sources. ~6 hours/week of repetitive manual aggregation.

Pain 2 · Inconsistency

Different analysts write briefs in different formats. Manager spends time figuring out what changed, not what matters.

Pain 3 · Anomaly blindness

"Looks fine" is the default when the analyst is rushing. Real anomalies get missed until end-of-week or end-of-month.

Pain 4 · Vacation collapse

When the analyst is out, the brief stops. Manager either flies blind or asks 3 different people for partial data.

Pain 5 · Career stagnation

The analyst becomes the briefing machine. Strategic projects keep getting bumped. Promotion velocity slows.

Pain 6 · Knowledge loss

The "how" of the brief lives in the analyst's head. When they leave, the institutional rhythm leaves with them.

10 · Buying Triggers & Objections

Top 5 Buying Triggers

  • New manager joins, asks "where's my morning brief?"
  • Analyst leaves; manager realises the brief was 100% in their head
  • Quarterly all-hands prep — manager wants a clean 90-day summary
  • An anomaly got missed → "this shouldn't happen again" mandate
  • Analyst hits burnout/notice — retention manager pays $40/mo to make their day easier

Top 5 Objections

  • "Can't we just use ChatGPT on our Sheets?" → Answer: not across sources, not on a schedule, not with anomaly memory.
  • "Security — you'll have access to our financial data" → Answer: read-only connectors, SOC2 on roadmap, customer-controlled scope.
  • "What if the AI hallucinates a number?" → Answer: every figure in the brief links back to the source row; the brief is a summary, not a synthesis.
  • "We already pay for Hex/Looker" → Answer: OpsBrief sits on top, not instead. $20–$40 increment, not a replacement.
  • "My analyst will see this as a threat" → Answer: gives them time back for higher-leverage work, not replaces them.

12 · Risk Assessment

HIGH

Integration Breadth

Building reliable read-connectors for Sheets, Snowflake, BigQuery, Looker, Slack is 70% of the engineering cost. Each new connector is a long tail of edge cases.

HIGH

Incumbent Squeeze

Google adding Gemini summaries to Sheets, or Hex/Looker adding native daily briefs, could collapse the wedge. Window may be 18–24 months.

MED

Trust on Numbers

One hallucinated metric in a CFO's brief and the product dies in that account. Requires source-link-back design from Day 1.

MED

GTM Channel

SMB analyst persona is hard to reach at scale. Likely channels: LinkedIn content, founder-led sales, communities (r/analytics, Locally Optimistic, Operators Guild).

LOW

Solo-founder Burnout

Founder is already employed (internship) + grad student. Calendar load is real. Mitigated by 90-day discovery-only sprint before any build commitment.

LOW

Demand

Pain is recurring and felt by the founder. Discovery risk is low; the unknown is willingness-to-pay at $40/mo (vs $0 status quo).

13 · Pivot Opportunities

Pivot 1 · Manager-side

If analysts won't pay, sell to their managers instead. "$200/mo per team, every analyst gets briefing automation." Higher ACV, slower sales cycle, but procurement-defensible.

Pivot 2 · Vertical

Focus only on one industry (e.g., e-commerce ops) and ship vertical-specific KPI templates. Smaller TAM, much faster GTM.

Pivot 3 · Consumer-of-one

Sell directly to individual analysts as a personal productivity tool, no team or admin gates. $15/mo, viral PLG via LinkedIn.

14 · Go / No-Go Recommendation

GOCONDITIONAL

Recommendation: Proceed with a 90-day validation sprint. Do not build the platform until 25 customer-discovery calls and 10 letters-of-intent are in hand. If 10 prospects say they would pay $20/mo for a no-code MVP brief delivered to their Slack, then commit to the build.

  • Founder-market fit (78/100) is high enough to invest founder time
  • Pain is daily, recurring, and observable — easy to validate in 30-min calls
  • Market is bootstrap-realistic — $2.4M ARR Y3 at 0.65% SAM penetration
  • Wedge is real — no incumbent owns the SMB-daily-briefing use case today
  • Window is closing — 18–24 months before Google/Hex collapse the wedge
